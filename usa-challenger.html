Overview

▾

The Space Shuttle Challenger was the second Space Shuttle orbiter, with its maiden flight in April 1983. On 28 January 1986, during its 10th launch, Challenger broke apart 73 seconds after liftoff, killing all seven crew members. The disaster was caused by the failure of O-ring seals in the solid rocket boosters, compromised by unusually cold temperatures.

The organisational story is mainly one of accomplishment rather than failure — embedded signals that became clear only in hindsight, near misses, close calls, and processes likely to recur. A system of organisational routines and can-do spirit “camouflaged a cumulative directionality” in signals that the O-rings were not performing as designed.

Columbia, the first orbiter, disintegrated during atmospheric re-entry on 1 February 2003, killing all seven astronauts. Discovery became the oldest surviving orbiter, completing its final mission on 24 February 2011.

Timeline & Location

▾

1972–1982: Initial shuttle development (~$10.6B, ~$49B in 2020 dollars). April 1983: Challenger maiden flight. 28 Jan 1986: Challenger disaster. 1 Feb 2003: Columbia disaster. 24 Feb 2011: Discovery final mission. Location: Kennedy Space Center, Merritt Island, Florida, USA.

Hazards

▾

Exogenous Hazards

Space debris, solar flares, terrestrial temperature extremes, cyber threat.

Endogenous Hazards

Spacecraft configuration, materials, extreme heat at lift-off. O-ring performance degradation in cold temperatures.

📊

Cost & Benefit

▾

Cost: Total Life-Cycle Cost approximately $209–255 billion. Initial development ~$10.6B (1972–82), ~$49B in 2020 dollars. Cost per flight ~$766M; average $1.2B per launch. Original target of weekly flights at $20M each was never achieved.

Benefit: Space exploration, Hubble Space Telescope, scientific research. Goal of safe, frequent and affordable access to space.

🛡

Resilience Principles Assessment

▾

Shared Responsibility (P5)

Critical organisational failures identified. No precedent for a contractor to recommend NASA not launch. No precedent for engineers to argue from intuitions rather than data, or for temperature to be cited as a variable. Top management’s insistence on explicit arguments silenced tacit reservations. Organisational routines “camouflaged a cumulative directionality” in signals that O-rings were failing.

Proactively Protected (P2) To Do

Consequences of re-use of the shuttle — accounted for adequately?

Environmentally Integrated (P3) To Do

No evidence identified.

Socially Engaged (P4)

Public curiosity about space and appreciation of high risks of space exploration.

Adaptively Transforming (P6)

Key failures: Engineers hadn’t dealt with cold conditions before; managers needed reason NOT to launch; engineers gave up trying to convince managers; cascade effect to entire shuttle programme. Similar failures with Columbia — managers lacked engineering appreciation to connect risks. Critical question: who is the risk management owner?

Continuously Learning (P1)

Columbia disaster (2003): insulating foam struck left wing during launch, weakening thermal protection. On re-entry, superheated air penetrated the wing causing breakup. Both mechanical and organisational causes needed addressing. The organisational failures of Challenger were repeated with Columbia.

🔮

Futures

▾

Lessons from both Challenger and Columbia disasters continue to inform space programme safety culture and organisational risk management practices.
