# Sections
sections:
  - id: overview
    icon: waves
    content: |
      The **Thames Barrier** is the world's second-largest movable flood barrier, spanning 520 metres across the River Thames at Woolwich in southeast London. It consists of **10 steel gates**, each of which can be rotated into position to block incoming tidal surges and protect the capital from flooding.

      Since becoming operational in **1984**, the Barrier has been closed over 200 times to protect London. Originally designed primarily for tidal surge events, its role has expanded to include management of combined tidal and fluvial (river) flood risk as climate change drives rising sea levels and more intense rainfall events.

      The Barrier protects an estimated **1.4 million properties** and **GBP 320 billion** worth of assets across the Thames floodplain, including critical national infrastructure such as the Houses of Parliament, hospitals, transport networks, and utilities.

  - id: timeline
    content: |
      The impetus for the Thames Barrier came from the **North Sea flood of 1953**, which killed over 300 people in England and prompted a national review of flood defences. The Greater London Council commissioned a feasibility study in the 1960s, and construction began in **1974**.

      The Barrier was completed and officially opened by **Queen Elizabeth II on 8 May 1984**. The Thames Estuary 2100 (TE2100) Plan, published in **2012**, set out a long-term adaptive strategy for managing tidal flood risk through to the end of the century and beyond, with the Barrier as its centrepiece.

      Located at **Woolwich, southeast London**, the Barrier sits at a strategic narrowing point of the Thames estuary.

  - id: stakeholders
    content: |
      The Barrier is owned and operated by the **Environment Agency**, an executive non-departmental public body of the UK government's Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra). Day-to-day operations are managed from the Thames Barrier Navigation Centre.

      Key stakeholders include the **Greater London Authority**, the **Port of London Authority**, riparian London boroughs, emergency services, and utility companies whose infrastructure is protected by the Barrier.

  - id: digitalisation
    content: |
      The Thames Barrier has undergone progressive **digitalisation** since its original commissioning. Early mechanical and hydraulic control systems have been supplemented and in some cases replaced by modern **SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition)** systems, real-time tidal and meteorological monitoring, and predictive flood modelling.

      The Environment Agency uses **data-driven surge forecasting** in collaboration with the Met Office to determine closure decisions, typically with 12-36 hours of lead time. The **TE2100 Plan** represents a pioneering example of adaptive management, using scenario-based planning to sequence investment decisions over a 100-year horizon based on observed climate data.

  - id: hazards
    type: hazards
    exogenous: "North Sea tidal surges driven by storm systems; rising sea levels due to climate change; extreme fluvial flows from upstream rainfall events."
    endogenous: "Finite mechanical design life of original components; increasing closure frequency raising questions about long-term capacity; land subsidence in southeast England."

  - id: cost-benefit
    content: |
      **Cost:** Original construction cost approximately **GBP 534 million** (at 1984 prices). Ongoing maintenance and operation costs are borne by the Environment Agency. The TE2100 Plan estimates total investment of **GBP 4 billion** over the century for the wider Thames estuary flood defence system.

      **Key Benefits:** The Barrier protects over **1.4 million properties** and **GBP 320 billion** in assets. Each closure prevents potential flood damages that could run into billions of pounds. The adaptive management approach of TE2100 allows investment to be staged and adjusted as climate science evolves, avoiding premature large-scale expenditure.

  - id: futures
    content: |
      The **Thames Estuary 2100 (TE2100) Plan** provides the strategic framework for the Barrier's future. It identifies decision points linked to sea level rise thresholds: if sea levels rise beyond certain bounds, options range from refurbishing the existing Barrier, to constructing a new barrier further downstream, to building a full estuary barrage.

      The current design life of the Barrier extends to approximately **2070**, but the TE2100 adaptive approach means that major investment decisions will be triggered by observed climate data rather than fixed dates. This makes the Thames Barrier one of the most sophisticated examples of **long-horizon adaptive infrastructure planning** globally.
