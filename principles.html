Principles of Resilient Infrastructure

A framework for assessing how infrastructure systems are designed, governed, and maintained to withstand and adapt to disruption.

Each case study in the DIR Wiki is assessed against the Principles of Resilient Infrastructure. These principles provide a consistent lens for evaluating how infrastructure systems anticipate, absorb, adapt to, and recover from hazards and disruptions.

The principles are not hierarchical — resilient infrastructure typically demonstrates strength across all six dimensions, though the balance varies by context, sector, and geography.

🏛
Accountability
Clear ownership, governance structures, and lines of responsibility for infrastructure performance. Leaders take a portfolio approach to risk and plan across long time horizons, ensuring transparent decision-making.

Protected by Design
Infrastructure is engineered with redundancy, robustness, and fail-safe mechanisms built into its physical design. Systems are sized and specified to handle both anticipated loads and extreme scenarios.

Environmentally Integrated
Infrastructure works with natural systems rather than against them. Design maximises resource efficiency, minimises environmental impact, and leverages natural processes such as gravity flow and energy recovery.

Socially Engaged
Communities and stakeholders are meaningfully involved in planning, design, and governance of infrastructure. The system serves equitable outcomes and builds public trust through engagement and transparency.

Adaptive Management
Systems and their governance can evolve in response to new information, changing conditions, and emerging risks. Decision-making processes support iteration and course correction over the infrastructure lifecycle.

Continual Learning
Lessons from operations, failures, near-misses, and peer systems are systematically captured and applied. Organisations cultivate knowledge-sharing cultures and invest in monitoring, data, and research.
